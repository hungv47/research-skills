# Initiative Types: Hero vs Support

Understanding the distinction between Hero and Support initiatives helps with resource allocation, expectation setting, and portfolio balance.

## Hero Initiatives

**Definition**: Major campaigns that drive significant impact and require substantial resources.

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Hero Initiative |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Scale** | Large reach, broad impact |
| **Resources** | Significant budget, multiple team members |
| **Timeline** | Weeks to months of execution |
| **Visibility** | High-profile, often cross-functional |
| **Risk** | Higher stakes, more to lose |
| **Ownership** | Dedicated owner, often with supporting team |

### When to Use Hero Initiatives

- Launching a new product or major feature
- Entering a new market or segment
- Major brand campaigns
- Strategic partnerships
- Seasonal tentpole events (Black Friday, product anniversaries)
- Turnaround efforts for underperforming metrics

### Examples

| Industry | Hero Initiative Example |
|----------|------------------------|
| **E-commerce** | Holiday mega-sale campaign with influencer partnerships |
| **SaaS** | Product Hunt launch with coordinated PR push |
| **Fintech** | Referral program launch with bonus incentives |
| **Gaming** | New season launch with content creator activations |
| **B2B** | Annual user conference with keynote announcements |

### Hero Initiative Checklist

Before classifying as Hero, confirm:
- [ ] Budget exceeds normal operating range
- [ ] Requires coordination across 2+ teams
- [ ] Timeline spans multiple sprints/cycles
- [ ] Success significantly moves key metrics
- [ ] Failure would be noticeable to leadership
- [ ] Has dedicated owner with authority to make decisions

---

## Support Initiatives

**Definition**: Smaller, complementary efforts that enhance or extend Hero initiatives, or provide incremental improvements.

### Characteristics

| Aspect | Support Initiative |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Scale** | Targeted reach, focused impact |
| **Resources** | Modest budget, 1-2 team members |
| **Timeline** | Days to weeks of execution |
| **Visibility** | Lower-profile, often single-team |
| **Risk** | Lower stakes, easier to adjust or kill |
| **Ownership** | Single owner, minimal dependencies |

### When to Use Support Initiatives

- Testing new channels or tactics before scaling
- Extending successful campaigns
- Filling gaps in the funnel
- Quick wins to maintain momentum
- Experimentation and learning
- Routine optimization efforts

### Examples

| Industry | Support Initiative Example |
|----------|---------------------------|
| **E-commerce** | A/B test of checkout button copy |
| **SaaS** | Email drip sequence optimization |
| **Fintech** | Push notification timing experiment |
| **Gaming** | In-app tooltip for underused feature |
| **B2B** | LinkedIn content series on specific pain point |

### Support Initiative Checklist

Before classifying as Support, confirm:
- [ ] Can be executed by single owner
- [ ] Budget within normal operating range
- [ ] Timeline fits within single sprint/cycle
- [ ] Failure won't materially impact key metrics
- [ ] Can be killed quickly if not working
- [ ] Minimal cross-team dependencies

## Comparison Matrix

| Factor | Hero | Support |
|--------|------|---------|
| Budget | $10K+ | <$10K |
| Duration | 4+ weeks | 1-3 weeks |
| Team size | 3+ people | 1-2 people |
| Approval level | Leadership | Manager |
| Risk tolerance | Lower (more to lose) | Higher (can fail fast) |
| Success impact | Material metric change | Incremental improvement |
| Planning depth | Detailed, documented | Lightweight, flexible |

*Note: Thresholds vary by company size and industry. Adjust based on your context.*

## Portfolio Balance

A healthy initiative portfolio includes both types:

**Recommended Mix**
- 2-3 Hero initiatives per quarter
- 5-10 Support initiatives per quarter

**Why Both Matter**
- Heroes drive step-change growth
- Supports enable learning and optimization
- Supports can graduate to Heroes if proven
- Heroes need Supports for full funnel coverage

**Anti-Pattern: All Heroes**
- Resource exhaustion
- No room for experimentation
- Higher aggregate risk

**Anti-Pattern: All Support**
- Death by a thousand paper cuts
- No big bets, no big wins
- Optimization without strategy

## Misclassification Risks

### Hero Disguised as Support
**Problem**: Underestimating resources needed leads to failure.
**Signs**:
- "Quick" project that keeps expanding
- Multiple teams getting pulled in
- Timeline keeps slipping
- Stakes higher than initially thought

**Fix**: Reclassify and properly resource, or descope to true Support.

### Support Disguised as Hero
**Problem**: Over-engineering simple experiments wastes resources.
**Signs**:
- Extensive planning for small test
- Multiple approval layers for minor change
- Treating pilot like full launch

**Fix**: Simplify planning, reduce approval layers, embrace iteration.
